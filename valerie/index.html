    💬Messages — the 4-touch sequence▶
    Rules first: never pitch in the connection request. No links until they ask. Short — every message under 60 words. The goal is a conversation, not a demo booking on message one.

    Connection request: default blank. 2026 data shows no-note requests get accepted more (≈38%) than short generic notes (≈29%). Only add a note if it's truly specific to them (a post of theirs, a shared event) — those hit ~45%.

    Swap [name] and [brand] before sending. Copy buttons grab the whole message.

    Touch 1 — after they accept
    Day 0–1
    Thanks for connecting, [name]. I talk to a lot of ops people at fashion brands — most tell me their Monday trading numbers take someone half a day to pull together across Shopify, wholesale and stores. Out of curiosity, how does that work at [brand]?
    Touch 2 — add value, don't chase
    Day 4–5, if no reply
    One thing I keep hearing from multi-channel brands: the data exists, it's just spread across a dozen systems. Coolr (smart vending, thousands of consumer touchpoints) had a report that took a full day — with Milo it's under an hour. Happy to share how if useful.
    Touch 3 — the honest one
    Day 10–12, if no reply
    Last one from me, [name] — I work at Milo, we let teams ask questions of their data in plain English (Slack/WhatsApp) instead of waiting on analysts. If getting answers out of your numbers is ever painful, worth 20 minutes. If not, no hard feelings.
    Touch 4 — the breakup
    Day 21+
    No response needed — I'll stop here. If reporting pain ever hits the top of the pile, you know where I am. Enjoyed following [brand]'s [recent thing] btw.
    If they reply at any point: answer like a human, ask about their setup, and aim for one thing — a 20-minute call. Then book it and log it.
    ✍️Content — rules & post structures▶

    Your lane

    You don't have fashion customer stories yet, so your lane is what you're hearing and seeing: fashion/e-comm ops and data pain. Observations from calls, industry takes, questions. Borrow Milo proof from named consumer brands — Coolr (day-long report → under an hour), Two Tribes Brewery (drinks/FMCG), Perlas (weeks of analysis → 2 minutes) — without over-claiming fashion experience. When the reference videos land, each one is a post.

    Naming rule: Coolr, Two Tribes, Perlas and ULG are fine to name. Nikki Beach and anything mid-deal stays anonymous ("a global beach club group") until Tom signs off.

    Beginner-friendly post structures (rotate)

    1. Something I heard this week: "I asked a Head of Ecommerce how they track sell-through across channels. The answer: three exports and a prayer." + 3–4 short lines on why + end with a question
    2. The observation: one specific, uncomfortable truth about fashion ops (returns data, wholesale vs DTC reporting gaps, WSSI spreadsheet hell) + question
    3. The learning-in-public post: "Three weeks into talking to fashion ops teams, here's what surprised me" — beginners get points for honesty. This was post #1.

    Non-negotiable rules (proven on Tom's account and algorithm-tested)

    • Best line goes FIRST. Hook in lines 1–2
    • Short sentences. Line breaks. Sound like you talking, not a blog
    • No corporate words: never "leverage", "transformative", "landscape", "seamless"
    • End with an experience question ("how does your team actually do this?") — never a pitch or "follow for more"
    • No links in the post body (~60% reach penalty) — links go in first comment
    • Post Tue/Wed/Thu only. Zero hashtags (no-hashtag posts now outperform by 5–10%). Under 2,500 characters
    • Reply to every comment in the first hour — substantive replies, not "Thanks!"
    • Stay on ONE topic lane (fashion ops/data). The 2026 algorithm distributes by topic — consistency compounds, detours cost reach

    Tom reviews the first 4 posts before publishing; after that, spot-checks.

    What a good comment looks like

    ✅ Add something"Seeing this with wholesale too — brands can tell you yesterday's DTC sales but their stockist sell-through is 3 weeks behind. Is that gap closing anywhere?"
    ✅ Ask a real question"Curious how many teams actually reconcile returns data against this — everyone I speak to does it monthly at best."
    ❌ Never"Great post!", "So true 👏", "Thanks for sharing" — the algorithm flags these as low-value and humans ignore them

    A good comment is one or two sentences, adds an observation from your calls or asks something you actually want answered. If you couldn't have written it without reading the post, it's good.

    📒Swipe file & learning loop▶

    Nobody can show you a perfect example thread — you build your own reference library:

    • Swipe file: a running doc. Every day, save 1–2 posts that made you stop scrolling. One line each: why did it work? (the hook? a number? a story?) By August you'll have 30+ examples — that's your textbook, and it's better than any course because it's calibrated to your feed and your buyers
    • Also save the bad ones: DMs you receive from salespeople (you'll get plenty). Note what made you ignore them. Never do those things
    • Experiment log: from August, change ONE thing per week (a different touch-1 question, a different prospect seniority, posting time) and write down what happened. One variable at a time or you learn nothing
    • Weekly self-review (before the Tuesday with Tom): 3 questions — what got replies this week? what would I not send again? what's one thing I'll try next week? Bring the answers, not just the numbers
    🚧Guardrails▶
    • LinkedIn limits: stay under ~25 connection requests/day and ~100/wk. New-ish accounts get throttled harder — start at 10/day and earn the volume. Withdraw pending requests older than 3 weeks (a big pending pile throttles you)
    • Pricing questions in a thread → keep it simple and hand to Tom if it goes deep; the DM's job is the meeting, not the negotiation
    • Gaming leads → Tom → Andy. Don't work them
    • Don't argue in comments, don't post on weekends, don't buy engagement pods (algorithm-flagged)
    • If a thread goes technical (warehouse, security), pull in Tom rather than winging it — "customer data never leaves your warehouse" and EU hosting are the anchor lines
