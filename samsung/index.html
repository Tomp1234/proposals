03
Section 03 / 08Inside

Your data might be messy, siloed, won't join up. Milo interacts with it.

You said the warehouse exists but very little of it joins up, sell-in and sell-out don't gel, the dashboards live in Qlik Sense, and the business is siloed enough that the left hand doesn't see the right. That isn't a reason Milo won't work. It's the reason it's worth doing. If the data were clean, you wouldn't need us.

We have never met a company with data clean and in one place. It does not happen. The companies worth solving for are the ones with ten or twenty sources that don't match at all, which is exactly where the value sits, because nobody else can get into it.

So data preparation isn't a precondition we hand back to your team. It's part of what Milo does. It writes the code to reconcile sell-in against sell-out, resolves the same product and customer across systems that name them differently, and holds Samsung's own definitions so a question means the same thing twice.

And every answer is built on Samsung's data only, never the open web, with the source and the rows behind it. The number you take into a room is one you can stand behind.

Section 04 / 08Engineered for Samsung

It runs inside your wall. Nothing leaves it.

You were direct: the policy is to upload nothing to any LLM, even Gauss, and a tool that holds Samsung's data and can't be removed is a non-starter. So Milo was built the other way round. Read it as a spec sheet.

Deployment
Into Samsung's own AWS, GCP or Azure account, or fully on-prem and offline. It lives in your tenancy, locked up. No row passes back out to us.
Model
Point Milo at your own connection to Azure OpenAI, or at Gauss, inside the tenancy. Many tools can only call out to OpenAI. Milo doesn't have to call anything out.
Data buckets
Proprietary and embargo, market research, and bought-in subscription data all handled the same way, because none of it leaves.
Training
Samsung's data answers Samsung's questions. Full stop. It never trains anything, and the contract says so.
Surface
White-labelled as your own internal tool. Your name on it, your perimeter around it.
The objection you raised at the end: “you become beholden to them.” You won't be. The deployment lives in Samsung's tenancy, and a code-escrow arrangement sits behind it, so if Milo went away tomorrow the application keeps running and Samsung keeps it. That is also the honest answer to the startup question: self-hosted means there is no plug for anyone to pull.

The numbers engine inside your tool.

Milo is for tabular, numeric data. You can drop a deck in as context and pull numbers from it, but it isn't a PDF-search tool. The harder problem, the numbers, is the one we solve.

Outputs that recombine
